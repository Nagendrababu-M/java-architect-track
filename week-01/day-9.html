WHAT THIS DAY IS

This week you learned eight things in isolation — the stack/heap split, class loading & reflection, reachability & generational GC, GC algorithms & tuning, the memory model, profiling, and modern Java. Real incidents don’t arrive labelled by topic. A page that says “p99 is spiking” could be GC, allocation, a lock, an I/O stall, a leak, or a memory-visibility bug — and the architect’s job is to triangulate across all eight domains to find which. Today is that integration: how the pieces combine into a single diagnostic discipline. Synthesis is exactly what an interviewer probes to tell a strong senior from a staff-level engineer.

The one mental model that unifies the week. Every Week-1 question ultimately reduces to: where does this data live, how long does it live, and who can see it? Stack vs heap (Day 1) and statics vs Metaspace (Day 2) answer where. Reachability and generational GC (Days 3–4) answer how long. The memory model (Day 5) answers who can see it, and when. Profiling (Day 6) is how you observe all of it under real load. Modern Java (Days 7–8) is how you write code that gets these right by construction — immutability, records, the right concurrency primitive. Hold that and no incident is “mysterious”; it’s just a question you haven’t pointed the right tool at yet.

STAGE 2 30 MIN

Walkthrough 1 — “We’re missing fills at market open”

INCIDENT

A trading service drops orders for 2–4 seconds right at market open, every day. p50 latency is fine; p99 is catastrophic during the window. CPU and average memory look normal on the dashboard.

Hypothesis DAY 6 — p99-only spikes with normal averages scream pauses, not steady load. Pauses come from GC or lock contention. Average-memory-normal doesn’t exonerate GC, because a burst of allocation at open can trigger a Full GC that the daily average hides.

Step 1 — pull the GC log DAY 4. You see, right at 09:15, a Pause Full (Ergonomics) of 2,800ms that freed almost nothing — or a cluster of long Young pauses. A multi-second Stop-the-World during the highest-traffic second of the day is the dropped-fills window.

Step 2 — why is GC firing then? DAY 3 Market open is an allocation storm: a flood of order objects created at once. If Young Gen is too small, those objects are promoted to Old Gen before they can die (the weak generational hypothesis working against you), Old Gen fills, and a Full GC fires. Confirm with an allocation profile DAY 6 — an allocation flame graph shows the order-parsing path churning the heap.

Step 3 — the fix DAY 4. Three levers: (1) size Young Gen so the open’s burst dies in Eden (-XX:NewSize/MaxNewSize from the allocation-rate calculation); (2) if the SLA is latency-critical, switch the collector — G1 at 200ms is still 200ms of missed prices, so ZGC (sub-ms pauses) is the architect’s call for a trading engine; (3) reduce allocation at the source DAY 7 — the allocation flame graph may reveal a stream pipeline boxing millions of Integers where an IntStream belongs, or per-order regex recompilation.

The synthesis: the dashboard (averages) lied; the GC log (Day 4) told the truth; reachability theory (Day 3) explained why; the allocation profiler (Day 6) located the source; and the fix spanned tuning (Day 4) and code (Day 7). No single day solved it.

QUICK CHECK

Why does “average memory looks normal” fail to rule out a GC problem here? Tie your answer to what an average hides versus what a GC log shows.

STAGE 3 25 MIN

Walkthrough 2 — “It OOMs every few days”

INCIDENT

A service runs fine for 2–3 days, then heap usage climbs steadily until OutOfMemoryError: Java heap space and a crash-loop. A restart resets the clock.

Hypothesis DAY 4 — a slow, monotonic climb to OOM with Full GCs that free less and less is the signature of a memory leak, not a sizing problem. A bigger heap only delays it. (Contrast: OutOfMemoryError: Metaspace would instead point at class-loading leaks DAY 2 — dynamic proxies, repeated redeploys, a classloader that never unloads.)

Step 1 — capture the evidence DAY 6. Set -XX:+HeapDumpOnOutOfMemoryError -XX:HeapDumpPath=... so the next OOM writes a heap dump automatically; or take one live with jcmd <PID> GC.heap_dump while usage is high.

Step 2 — analyze DAY 6. Open the .hprof in Eclipse MAT; the “Leak Suspects” report ranks objects by retained heap. Follow the path to GC roots DAY 3 to find what reference is keeping the leak alive — that’s the whole game: the GC is working correctly; something reachable shouldn’t be.

Step 3 — the usual culprit DAY 2. The path almost always ends at a static collection that only ever grows — an unbounded cache, a listener list nobody deregisters, a Map keyed by something unbounded. Statics live for the life of the classloader, so anything they hold is permanently reachable.

Step 4 — the fix DAY 7/DAY 8. Bound the cache (Caffeine/Guava with maximumSize or expireAfterAccess); deregister listeners; or redesign so the data isn’t held at all. Prefer immutable, clearly-scoped state — an architect’s default that makes leaks structurally harder.

🎯 INTERVIEW SIGNAL

“How do you find a memory leak?” — Heap dump on OOM → Eclipse MAT → sort by retained heap → path to GC roots → identify the unintended strong reference (usually a static/unbounded collection) → bound or remove it. Naming “retained heap” and “path to GC roots” is the senior tell.

QUICK CHECK

Two services crash with OOM. One says Java heap space, the other Metaspace. Which Week-1 day owns each, and what fundamentally different thing is leaking in each case?

STAGE 4 25 MIN

Walkthrough 3 — “The numbers are sometimes wrong”

INCIDENT

A position-tracking counter is occasionally off by a few. Worse, one node sometimes serves a config value that another node updated minutes ago. No exceptions, no crash — just quietly wrong, only under load, never reproducible in a single-threaded test.

Hypothesis DAY 5 — “correct single-threaded, wrong under load, no exception” is the fingerprint of a data race: conflicting accesses with no happens-before edge. There are two distinct sub-bugs here, and conflating them is the classic mistake.

Bug 1 — lost updates (atomicity) DAY 5. The off-by-a-few counter is count++ — read-modify-write, not atomic. Two threads read the same value and both write back, losing an increment. volatile would not fix this (it gives visibility, not atomicity). The fix is AtomicLong.incrementAndGet() (a CAS) or a lock.

Bug 2 — stale config (visibility) DAY 5. A plain field updated by one thread may never become visible to another — there’s no happens-before edge publishing the write. Make the field volatile (visibility + ordering), or guard it with a lock. If the config is an object, publish it safely — an immutable snapshot in a volatile/AtomicReference field, swapped atomically.

The architect-grade fix DAY 8. Model config as an immutable record and hold it in a single volatile reference; updates create a new record and reassign. Now every reader either sees the old snapshot or the new one, never a torn mix, and there’s no per-field synchronization. Immutability (Day 5’s safe-publication payoff) + records (Day 8) makes the whole class of visibility bugs disappear by construction.

🎯 INTERVIEW SIGNAL

“Is this code thread-safe?” — Separate the three guarantees out loud: is each shared access atomic, visible, and ordered? Name the missing happens-before edge. Propose the minimal correct primitive (atomic for counters, volatile/immutable snapshot for config, lock for compound invariants). That structured answer is the staff-level signal.

QUICK CHECK

A teammate “fixes” the off-by-a-few counter by marking it volatile. Why does the bug persist, and what does volatile actually fix in this incident?

STAGE 5 25 MIN

Walkthrough 4 — “CPU is fine but throughput halved”

INCIDENT

After a release, a service handles half its previous load. CPU is not pegged. The GC log is clean. Latency under concurrency is far worse than the per-request work suggests it should be.

Hypothesis DAY 6 — idle CPU + clean GC + bad-under-concurrency rules out CPU-bound and GC, and points at off-CPU time: locks or thread/pool starvation. A CPU flame graph would look misleadingly flat; reach for thread dumps and wall-clock profiling instead.

Path A — common-pool starvation DAY 7. Thread dumps show request threads WAITING on the ForkJoinPool.commonPool. The release added a parallelStream() inside the request handler; under concurrent load every request fights for the tiny shared pool, and they queue. Fix: drop the parallelism (the server already parallelizes across requests), or isolate it on a dedicated pool. This is the Day 7 common-pool trap in the wild.

Path B — virtual-thread pinning DAY 8. The service moved to virtual threads expecting blocking I/O to scale, but throughput barely improved and carriers sit idle. -Djdk.tracePinnedThreads=full shows threads pinned inside a synchronized block wrapping a slow external call — pinned virtual threads can’t unmount, so the carrier is wasted. Fix: replace synchronized with ReentrantLock on that path.

Path C — the regex hot path DAY 6. If CPU were pegged, the flame graph would show a wide Pattern.compile leaf — a regex recompiled per request. Fix the call site (a static final Pattern), not the leaf.

The synthesis: the shape of the evidence (idle CPU, clean GC) chose the tool (thread dump / wall-clock, not a CPU profile), and the root cause lived in modern-Java concurrency (Days 7–8). Picking the wrong profiler mode here — a CPU flame graph — would have shown nothing and burned a day.

The capstone — one incident, all eight days

The Week-1 final. A single messy incident that touches multiple domains at once — exactly like real life. Read it, then answer all three parts (50+ characters each) and reveal the model answers. Reason like an architect: classify, hypothesize, name the tool, name the day.

SCENARIO · READ CAREFULLY BEFORE ANSWERING

The order service that degrades over the trading day

System: a Java 17 order-matching service, G1GC, 8 GB heap, behind a 1-second gateway timeout.

Symptoms, observed together:

  • At market open, a burst of 2–3s unresponsiveness, then it recovers.
  • Over the trading day, p99 latency creeps upward and heap usage trends up; by close it’s near OOM and gets restarted nightly.
  • Intermittently, a position counter reports slightly wrong totals under peak load — no exceptions.
  • CPU is moderate throughout; thread dumps at peak show many threads WAITING, a few BLOCKED.
YOUR ANSWERS · MINIMUM 20 CHARS EACH TO UNLOCK MODEL ANSWER
0 / 20 characters
MODEL ANSWER

Four distinct problems. (1) Market-open 2–3s freeze — a GC pause driven by the open’s allocation storm (Day 4 GC logs, Day 3 reachability, Day 6 allocation profile). (2) Day-long heap creep to OOM — a memory leak, almost certainly an unbounded cache/static collection that grows with the day’s orders (Day 2 statics, Day 3 reachability, Day 6 heap dump + MAT). (3) Wrong counter under load — a data race / lost update on a non-atomic count++ (Day 5 JMM). (4) Many WAITING/BLOCKED threads at peak with moderate CPU — lock contention or pool/I/O starvation, not a CPU problem (Day 6 thread dumps; possibly Day 7 common-pool or Day 8 pinning). The key insight: resist forcing one root cause — the discipline is to split a multi-symptom incident into independent failures first.

MODEL ANSWER

Work cheapest-and-highest-impact first. (a) Enable GC logging and -XX:+HeapDumpOnOutOfMemoryError immediately — zero-cost, and both the open freeze and the OOM leave evidence you want captured before the next occurrence (Day 4/6). (b) Read the GC log at 09:15 to confirm the open pause and its type/duration; correlate with an allocation flame graph to find the churn source (Day 4/6/3). (c) After the next OOM, open the heap dump in MAT, sort by retained heap, follow the path to GC roots to the leaking collection (Day 6/2/3). (d) Take three thread dumps seconds apart at peak to classify the WAITING/BLOCKED threads — common pool? a specific monitor? pinning? (Day 6/7/8). (e) Code-review the counter for a non-atomic RMW (Day 5) — cheap to spot, no runtime evidence needed. Order rationale: turn on always-on evidence first so nothing is lost, then descend the diagnostic ladder from logs to dumps to profiles.

MODEL ANSWER

Open freeze: size Young Gen for the open’s allocation burst, and for a latency-critical engine switch to ZGC for sub-ms pauses; reduce allocation at the source if the profile shows boxing/regex churn (Day 4/7). Leak: bound the offending cache (Caffeine maximumSize/expireAfterAccess) or remove the unintended static reference (Day 2/7). Counter: replace count++ with AtomicLong.incrementAndGet (Day 5). Contention: if it’s the common pool, drop the in-request parallelStream or isolate it; if it’s pinning, swap synchronized for ReentrantLock (Day 7/8). The trap: “just give it a bigger heap.” A larger heap delays the OOM (hiding the leak until it returns) and can lengthen the market-open Full GC pause — making the freeze worse while masking the leak. Bigger heap is the intuitive move and the wrong one; it treats two different problems with a change that helps neither and hurts one.
