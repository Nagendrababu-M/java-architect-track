STAGE 1 10 MIN

Reachability — what “garbage” actually means

Most engineers think the GC tracks “objects that aren’t used anymore.” That’s wrong. The GC has no idea what “used” means. It tracks something much simpler and more mechanical: reachability.

An object is reachable if you can trace a chain of references to it, starting from a special set of starting points called GC Roots.

What counts as a GC Root?

  • Local variables in active stack frames — every method currently executing
  • Static fields — reachable from any loaded class in Metaspace
  • Active threads themselves
  • JNI references — native code holding Java objects

The GC starts from these roots, follows every reference outward, and marks everything it can reach as “live.” Anything it can’t reach is, by definition, garbage — even if your code might still “intend” to use it. There’s no way to use it because nothing points to it.

Look at X and Y on the right. They reference each other — from their perspective, they’re alive. But no GC root can reach them. They’re garbage. The GC will reclaim them.

Reachability is one-way and root-anchored.

An object being “referenced” isn’t enough to keep it alive. It must be referenced by something the GC can ultimately trace back to a root. This is why circular references don’t cause leaks in Java (unlike old C++ ref-counting systems).

STAGE 2 14 MIN

The two foundational algorithms

Every garbage collector ever written is built on combinations of two core algorithms. Understand these two and you understand every GC you’ll meet.

Algorithm 1 — Mark and Sweep

The naive, foundational approach:

  1. Mark phase: Walk from every GC root, follow references, mark every reachable object as “live.”
  2. Sweep phase: Walk through the entire heap. Anything not marked is garbage — reclaim its memory.

Simple. But it has a serious flaw: after the sweep, your heap looks like swiss cheese — reclaimed slots scattered between surviving objects. This is called memory fragmentation. Allocating a large new object becomes hard because no single contiguous slot is big enough, even if total free space is plenty.

Algorithm 2 — Copying (Stop and Copy)

Split the available memory into two halves: From-Space and To-Space. New objects are allocated into From-Space. When it fills up:

  1. Walk from GC roots. For every live object found, copy it into To-Space, packed tightly with no gaps.
  2. Once done, discard the entire From-Space (everything left there was garbage anyway).
  3. Swap the roles. To-Space becomes the new From-Space.

Copying is brilliant for two reasons: no fragmentation (live objects are packed tightly), and cost is proportional to live objects, not total objects. If 90% of your heap is garbage, copying only touches the 10% that’s alive — the dead 90% is “free” to skip.

The catch: you only use half your memory at any moment. If your heap is 4GB, only 2GB is usable. Wasteful — unless you can be clever about where you apply this algorithm.

STAGE 3 12 MIN

The generational hypothesis — the trick that changed everything

In the 1980s, GC researchers discovered something profound by measuring real programs:

The Weak Generational Hypothesis

Most objects die young. The vast majority of objects become garbage almost immediately after creation. If an object survives long enough to be checked twice, it’s likely to live a long time.

Think about your own Java code. A typical request handler creates: temporary strings, intermediate calculation results, throwaway lists, parser tokens, lambda captures — all of which die at the end of the method. Meanwhile, your application’s long-lived stuff (connection pools, caches, configuration) was allocated once at startup and lives forever.

It’s like a hospital. The ER is full of patients who arrive and leave within hours. The long-term ward has patients who’ve been there for years. If you ran a single “check on every patient” sweep across the whole hospital every hour, you’d be wasting time on the long-term residents who haven’t changed.

Smarter: check the ER every hour (high churn), check the long-term ward once a week (low churn).

The architectural insight

If most objects die young, then we should:

  • Use the copying algorithm for new objects — because it’s cheap when most things are garbage
  • Use a different strategy for survivors — objects that have lived a while will likely keep living, so don’t waste effort moving them repeatedly

This led to the generational heap — the design used by every mainstream JVM garbage collector. The heap is split into regions based on object age.

How objects flow through the generations

  1. Birth: Every new allocation goes into Eden.
  2. Eden fills: Triggers a Minor GC. Live objects copied from Eden to Survivor space (S0). Eden is wiped.
  3. Next Minor GC: Live objects from Eden + S0 are copied into S1. S0 and Eden are wiped. (S0 and S1 swap roles each cycle.)
  4. Aging: Each object has a counter tracking how many GCs it’s survived. When it crosses a threshold (typically ~15), it gets tenured — promoted to Old Gen.
  5. Old Gen fills (eventually): Triggers a Major GC — much more expensive, often using a mark-sweep-compact algorithm.

This is why young-gen GCs are fast (small region, copying algorithm, almost everything is garbage) and old-gen GCs are slow (large region, more sophisticated algorithm, lots of survivors to scan).

STAGE 4 10 MIN

Minor, Major, Full — and Stop-the-World

Three GC events you need to recognize instantly in production logs:

EventWhat it doesCost
Minor GC Collects only the young generation Fast (ms). Frequent. Usually fine.
Major GC Collects the old generation Slow (100ms–1s+). Less frequent. Pay attention.
Full GC Collects young + old + sometimes Metaspace Very slow (seconds). Rare. Always investigate.

Stop-the-World — the silent killer

Most GC phases require the application to pause completely while the GC works. Every application thread freezes. No requests served. No responses sent. No code executes.

This is called a Stop-the-World (STW) pause. Why is it necessary? Because if your app threads keep mutating references while the GC is trying to figure out what’s reachable, the GC’s answers would be wrong — it might collect something that just became reachable, or miss something that just became garbage.

STW pauses are the #1 source of latency spikes in production Java services.

Your p99 latency isn’t hurt by your code being slow. It’s hurt by your code being frozen for 200ms during a Major GC. Modern GCs (G1, ZGC, Shenandoah — Day 4) exist almost entirely to minimize STW pauses.

For your Nifty options engine: a 500ms Full GC during market open isn’t a “slow request.” It’s 500ms of not seeing prices. The market keeps moving while your JVM is frozen. Whatever signals you missed are gone. Your fill is worse. Your edge is gone.

This is why low-latency Java trading systems obsess over GC tuning. By Day 4, you’ll know how to size Young Gen so your Minor GCs stay under 5ms, and how to pick a GC algorithm that never does a Full GC during the trading window.

Key Takeaways
  • Minor GC is fast (single-digit ms) and happens frequently — it cleans the young generation. Major GC is slower (tens to hundreds of ms) and cleans the old generation.
  • The generational hypothesis is the foundation of modern GC: most objects die young. Your job as architect is to design systems that respect this hypothesis.
  • p50 latency can look fine while p99 is on fire. GC pauses live in the tail — always measure p99 and p999 for latency-sensitive services.
