Stage 3: Java Flight Recorder — always-on, production-safe profiling. JFR is the JVM's built-in event recorder. Since Java 11 it's free and open-source; it was a commercial feature in Java 8, which is a detail worth dropping in an interview. Its defining trait is near-zero overhead, typically under one percent — low enough to run continuously in production.

What makes JFR special is that it's event-based, not just a sampler. It captures a continuous ring buffer of thousands of event types: GC pauses, allocations, method samples, lock contention, thread parks, I/O, exceptions, JIT compilation, even your own custom application events. And because it's a ring buffer, you can dump the last N minutes after the incident already happened. That is the killer feature for intermittent problems you can't reproduce on demand — the evidence is already sitting in the buffer.

To use it: jcmd PID JFR.start with a duration and the profile settings writes a recording. Or enable it at startup with minus XX StartFlightRecording, disk equals true, with a max size, running continuously, and then jcmd PID JFR.dump grabs the buffer right now. There are two built-in configs: default, under one percent overhead and safe everywhere, and profile, more detail at slightly higher overhead, fine for short investigations.

You analyze the dot jfr file in JDK Mission Control, JMC. The four views you check first map directly onto Stage 1's bottleneck classes. GC events: pause durations, frequency, heap before and after — confirms or kills the GC hypothesis. Memory and TLAB allocation: which classes and call sites allocate the most bytes; sustained climbs reveal leaks and churn. Method profiling, the hot methods or flame graph view: where CPU time goes. And lock instances and thread parks: which monitors block which threads, and for how long.

The bigger lesson is that continuous profiling is the modern standard. Always-on JFR — or a continuous profiler like Grafana Pyroscope or Parca, built on the same idea — means that when an alert fires at three in the morning, the evidence is already captured. You dump the buffer instead of trying to reproduce. "We'll add profiling and wait for it to happen again" is the slow, expensive path. Architects design observability in before the incident, not after.
