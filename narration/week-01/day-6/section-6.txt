Stage 6 is your capstone — diagnose from the flame graph. Let me set up the scenario, and you work it before revealing the model answers.

The system: a REST endpoint validating incoming trade payloads. Under load, CPU sits at a hundred percent, and throughput is half of what capacity planning predicted. The p99 is bad. The GC log is clean. And thread dumps show threads RUNNABLE, not blocked.

You pull a thirty-second CPU flame graph from async-profiler, where width is CPU time. At the bottom, validate takes ninety-eight percent. Above it, RuleEngine checkAll, ninety-four percent. Above that the time splits: rangeCheck takes about twelve percent, and matchSymbol takes about eighty percent. And almost all of matchSymbol is Pattern.compile at seventy-six percent, with the regex compilation internals as the widest leaf at the top, nothing significant above it.

First question: where is the CPU actually going, and how do you know from the shape, not the labels alone? Follow width up the tower. validate and checkAll are wide, but each has a wide child, so the time passes through them — they're not the culprit. The time splits at checkAll, and matchSymbol's eighty percent is almost entirely Pattern.compile at seventy-six, which is the widest leaf with nothing above it. The shape tells the story: the resource is consumed at the top of the widest tower, and that top is regex compilation. Seventy-six percent of all CPU is spent compiling regular expressions.

Second question: what's the actual bug, and the fix? The bug is in the caller, not in Pattern.compile. matchSymbol is calling Pattern.compile on every invocation — recompiling the same regex for every trade, when compilation is the expensive step and matching is cheap. The fix: compile each pattern once and reuse it. Hoist it to a private static final Pattern, or cache compiled patterns by key. Compiled Pattern objects are thread-safe for matching, so a static field is safe. This is the "hot leaf is a symptom, the bug is calling it too often" lesson from Stage 5 — you fix the call site, not the leaf. Expect CPU to drop dramatically and throughput to roughly double, matching the capacity prediction.

Third question: why were the clean GC log and the RUNNABLE threads essential? This is Stage 1's classification in action. CPU pegged with threads RUNNABLE, not BLOCKED or WAITING, rules out lock contention and I/O — the time is being spent computing, not waiting. A clean GC log rules out GC pauses. That triangulation is what justifies a CPU flame graph rather than wall-clock, lock, or allocation mode. Had threads been BLOCKED, a CPU profile would have looked misleadingly idle. Choosing the profiler mode from the symptom is the whole methodology.
