Stage 5: reading flame graphs. A flame graph is the standard visualization for a sampled profile, and once you can read one, diagnosis becomes fast and visual.

Four rules. First, each box is a stack frame — a method — and the box below called the box above. Second, and most important: width equals time, measured in sample count. A frame twice as wide consumed twice the resource. That is the only axis that means anything quantitatively. Third, the y-axis is stack depth, not time. The left-to-right ordering is alphabetical or merge order, not chronological — do not read a flame graph as a timeline; that's the number one beginner mistake. Fourth, look for wide plateaus. A wide box near the top with little above it is where the resource is actually spent — it's the leaf doing the work. Wide-but-deep towers are just call depth; the top of a wide stack is the culprit.

There are several variations worth knowing. A CPU flame graph: width is CPU time, finds compute hot spots. An allocation flame graph: width is bytes allocated, finds GC-pressure sources — straight back to Days 3 and 4. An off-CPU or wall-clock flame graph: width is time blocked or waiting, finds I/O and lock waits. A differential flame graph colors the change between two profiles, before and after a deploy — the fastest way to spot a regression, because the new hot frame lights up red. And an icicle graph is the same thing flipped, root on top; JMC uses that orientation.

The workflow ties it together: find the widest frame at the top of a tower — that's where the resource goes. Click to zoom. Read the call path below it to understand why it's hot. Then fix the call site, not the leaf. This last point is crucial: a method being hot is often just the symptom. The bug is usually the caller invoking it too often — for example, recompiling a regular expression on every single request. The leaf is innocent; the caller is guilty. Keep that in mind, because it's exactly the trap in the Stage 6 challenge.
