Stage 5 is your open-ended challenge. No auto-check here. No dropdowns. Just a scenario and three questions that require you to reason like an architect.

Here's the scenario. You're running a Java service handling five thousand requests per second. Average response time is eight milliseconds. The service has been stable for months.

This morning, something changed. Your p50 latency — the median — is still eight milliseconds, completely unchanged. But your p99 latency, which used to be a comfortable twenty-five milliseconds, is now spiking to three hundred and fifty milliseconds every forty-five seconds. No code was deployed. No traffic changes. But if you look at CPU at one-second resolution, you see brief spikes that align perfectly with the latency spikes.

You suspect garbage collection. Let me help you think through each of the three questions before you write your answers.

Question one: which type of GC event is most likely causing this?

Consider the duration first. Three hundred and fifty milliseconds. Minor GCs are single-digit milliseconds — way too short to cause a three hundred and fifty millisecond freeze. Full GCs would be longer and rarer. Three hundred and fifty milliseconds is a very good fit for a Major GC — collecting the old generation with a slower algorithm on a large set of live objects. Start there.

Question two: what does the forty-five second interval tell you?

The regularity of the interval is a clue about allocation behavior. If the old generation were filling due to random variation, the interval would be irregular. A consistent forty-five seconds suggests the old generation is accumulating at a steady, predictable rate. Something is being promoted from the young generation at a constant pace — which means something is surviving enough Minor GC cycles to get tenured. A growing cache is the most likely culprit. A session store. Connection state. Something long-lived that's slowly accumulating.

Question three: why does this matter if p50 is fine?

This is the question you'll be asked in every engineering review for the rest of your career. P50 is the median — it tells you about the average experience. But for a service doing five thousand requests per second, a three hundred and fifty millisecond spike every forty-five seconds affects roughly two hundred and twenty-five requests per spike. Those users don't see a slow service — they see what feels like a broken service. Buttons that don't respond. Requests that timeout. And then they retry — simultaneously — creating a thundering herd that hits your service right as it's coming back from the GC pause.

SLA contracts are almost always written in terms of p99 and p999. "p50 is fine" is not a defense if your p99 violates the contract. Write that argument down in your own words — you'll use it.
