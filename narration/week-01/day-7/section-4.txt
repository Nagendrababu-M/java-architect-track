Stage 4 covers two quality-of-life features that still carry interview-worthy details: text blocks and Sequenced Collections.

Text blocks first. Triple quotes, multi-line string, perfect for SQL and JSON. The detail everyone misses is incidental indentation stripping: the position of the closing triple-quote determines the left margin. Whitespace common to the left of it gets stripped; everything to the right is preserved. Move the closing delimiter left, and you've added indentation to every line. Two escapes worth knowing: a backslash at the end of a line suppresses the newline — one logical line split for readability — and backslash-s preserves a trailing space that would otherwise be stripped. And remember: a text block is still just a String. Same type, same methods, same constant pool.

Now Sequenced Collections, Java 21 — a fix for a twenty-year-old inconsistency. Getting the first element was spelled differently on every collection: list dot get zero, deque dot getFirst, sortedSet dot first. And LinkedHashSet — a collection whose entire purpose is maintaining order — had no way to get its last element without iterating the whole thing.

JEP 431 retrofits three interfaces. SequencedCollection gives every ordered collection getFirst, getLast, addFirst, addLast, removeFirst, removeLast, and reversed. SequencedSet covers LinkedHashSet and SortedSet. SequencedMap gives LinkedHashMap firstEntry, lastEntry, putFirst, putLast.

The headline method is reversed(). It returns a view, not a copy. Zero allocation, and mutations write through to the original. Iterating a LinkedHashMap backwards used to mean copying all the keys into a list and reversing it — now it's just map dot reversed.

Two interview details. First: what does addFirst do on a SortedSet? It throws UnsupportedOperationException — a sorted set's order comes from its comparator; you can't impose a position on it. The interface gives sorted collections the read methods, not positional writes. Second, the LRU trick: on a LinkedHashSet, addFirst with an element that already exists moves it to the front. So an LRU-style structure becomes: addFirst on access, removeLast to evict. Two method calls that used to require a hand-rolled data structure.
