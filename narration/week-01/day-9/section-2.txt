Stage 2: stream internals. The fact every candidate states and few can explain: streams are lazy. Let's make you one of the few.

A pipeline is a source, intermediate operations, and one terminal operation. Here's the key: intermediate operations do nothing. Filter doesn't filter. Map doesn't map. Each call just builds another stage in a linked chain of pipeline objects. All the work happens when the terminal operation pulls.

And when it pulls, the pipeline fuses. Each element flows through ALL the stages — filtered, mapped, accumulated — before the next element starts. There is no intermediate collection sitting between filter and map. One pass over the data, nothing materialized. That fusion is also what makes short-circuiting work: findFirst stops pulling the moment it has an answer. It's why an infinite stream with a filter and findFirst terminates — elements are produced one at a time, on demand, and demand stops.

Underneath every source is a Spliterator — a splittable iterator. Two jobs: tryAdvance pushes one element through the pipeline, and trySplit carves off a chunk for another thread — that's the engine of parallel streams. Spliterators also report characteristics: SIZED means it knows its count, ORDERED means encounter order matters, DISTINCT, SORTED, and so on. The pipeline exploits these to skip work: distinct on an already-distinct source is a no-op; toArray on a sized source preallocates exactly.

Now the distinction that controls performance: stateless versus stateful operations. Filter, map, flatMap — stateless. Each element is independent; they fuse perfectly and parallelize perfectly. Sorted, distinct, limit, skip — stateful. Sorted is the extreme case: it must see every element before emitting one, so everything upstream runs to completion into a buffer, the buffer sorts, then downstream resumes. A sorted in the middle of your pipeline breaks the one-pass model. In parallel pipelines, stateful operations are where scalability goes to die.

One more pair: flatMap versus mapMulti, Java 16. FlatMap allocates a whole Stream object per element — expensive when most elements expand to zero or one result. MapMulti hands you a consumer to push results into — no per-element allocation. Profile says flatMap is hot and the expansion is mostly zero-or-one? Reach for mapMulti.

Quick warnings for the interview. A stream is one-shot: the terminal operation consumes the spliterator, and a second terminal call throws IllegalStateException. And peek is not guaranteed to run — elements never pulled are never peeked, and the pipeline may elide work entirely. Never put required side effects in peek.
