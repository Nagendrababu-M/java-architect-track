Stage 3: contention and LongAdder — when CAS spins too much. CAS's weakness is its strength inverted. Under low contention it's near-free. But under high write contention — many threads hammering one AtomicLong — they all read the same value, all try to CAS, all but one fail, and all retry. The hot cache line ping-pongs between cores — that's cache-line bouncing — and throughput collapses, even though nobody is blocked. Spinning isn't free; it's wasted CPU.

The fix is LongAdder, and it beats AtomicLong for high-contention counters by not contending in the first place. LongAdder keeps an array of cells. Under contention, different threads update different cells, so their CAS operations rarely collide. The total is computed as the sum of all cells, on read. You've traded a single hot location for many cold ones. The cost: sum is not a perfectly-atomic instantaneous snapshot, and it's slightly slower than a single get. So the rule: LongAdder for write-heavy counters you mostly increment and rarely read — metrics, request counts. AtomicLong when you need the exact current value frequently, or when contention is low. LongAccumulator and DoubleAdder generalize this to other reductions.

And here's the nuance that separates seniors: the belief that lock-free is always faster is wrong. Under sustained extreme contention on a single value, a lock can actually outperform a CAS loop. Why? A lock puts the losers to sleep — one cache-line transfer, then quiet. CAS losers keep spinning and bouncing the cache line. So the architect's rule is: measure. Optimistic for low to moderate contention; striped structures like LongAdder, or even a lock, for the genuinely hot single point.

Interview-ready. LongAdder versus AtomicLong? LongAdder stripes across cells to avoid contention, summed on read — far faster for high-frequency increments, but sum isn't a precise atomic snapshot. AtomicLong is better for low contention or when you read the exact value often. And is lock-free always faster than locking? No — under extreme contention CAS loops spin and bounce the cache line while a lock parks the losers. Measure; sometimes a lock or a striped counter wins.
