Stage 6 is your capstone — from a coarse lock to a scalable design. Let me set up the scenario, then walk the model answers.

Here's a QuoteCache class. It holds a plain HashMap. Every method is synchronized: get returns map dot get, put calls map dot put, and size returns map dot size. Reads, the gets, outnumber writes, the puts, about ten thousand to one. Under market load, profiling shows threads piling up BLOCKED on this object's monitor, and throughput is capped far below CPU capacity.

First question: why is throughput capped despite reads not conflicting? Because every method is synchronized on the same monitor, the instance, so the lock serializes all access — including the ten thousand reads that don't conflict with each other at all. Only one thread can be inside any method at a time, so concurrent gets queue behind one another, even though reading a HashMap concurrently would be safe if nobody were writing. That queueing is exactly the BLOCKED pile-up in the profile. Throughput is capped at one read at a time — classic lock contention from coarse granularity, not a CPU or GC problem.

Second question: refactor it. The best first choice is to replace the whole thing with a ConcurrentHashMap and drop every synchronized — lock-free reads, fine-grained striped write locking, and a correct concurrent size, with zero hand-rolled locking. That's the architect's answer: prefer the proven concurrent collection over rolling your own. If you must keep a plain map — say for custom eviction — use a ReentrantReadWriteLock: get and size under the read lock, many concurrent readers; put under the write lock, exclusive. The correctness rule: every access, including reads, must go through the lock, or readers lose the visibility guarantee. With reads at ten thousand to one, a StampedLock with optimistic reads scales even better, at the cost of more complex, non-reentrant code. Order of preference: ConcurrentHashMap, then StampedLock, then ReadWriteLock, then the original.

Third question: a junior proposes "just make get non-synchronized for speed, keep put synchronized." Why is that wrong? It breaks visibility. The happens-before edge that publishes a writer's changes exists only between an unlock and a subsequent lock of the same monitor — Day 5. If the reader never acquires the lock, there's no edge from the writer's put to the reader's get, so the reader may see a stale value, a partially-updated HashMap — which can also corrupt or loop on resize — or no entry at all. Synchronizing only the writer is a classic half-fix: mutual exclusion among writers, but no visibility for readers. Both sides must synchronize on the same lock, or, better, use a structure whose reads carry their own happens-before guarantees.

That's Day 11. You now have the full locking toolkit — from the intrinsic monitor to explicit locks, conditions, read-write and stamped locks, and deadlock-free design. Next, Day 12: atomics, compare-and-swap, and lock-free patterns — thread-safety without locks at all.
