Stage 4: saturation policies and backpressure — what happens when you're full. When the pool is at max threads and the queue is full, the next task hits the rejection handler, the saturation policy. Choosing it is an architectural decision about how your service behaves under overload.

There are four built-in policies. AbortPolicy, the default, throws a RejectedExecutionException — fail fast, and the caller must handle or retry. CallerRunsPolicy runs the task on the submitting thread — this is backpressure, and we'll come back to it. DiscardPolicy silently drops the task — fine for lossy-OK work like best-effort metrics. And DiscardOldestPolicy drops the oldest queued task and retries the new one — good for latest-value-wins data, like live quotes.

Now the single most useful production combination: a bounded queue plus CallerRunsPolicy. When the pool saturates, the task runs on the caller's thread. Think about what that means — the thread that submits work is now busy doing work, so it can't submit more. The producer is automatically throttled to the rate the pool can sustain, without dropping anything, and without unbounded memory growth. The system slows down instead of falling over. That graceful degradation under overload is exactly what an architect designs for.

Contrast that with the alternative, an unbounded queue: it doesn't apply backpressure at all; it just absorbs the overload into memory until the JVM dies. "Slow down" is almost always better than "crash later."

So two interview-ready answers. How do you apply backpressure with a thread pool? A bounded queue plus CallerRunsPolicy: saturation pushes work back onto the producer thread, throttling intake to sustainable throughput without dropping tasks or growing memory. And when would you use DiscardOldestPolicy? When only the freshest data matters and stale queued tasks are worthless — live market quotes, sensor readings — so dropping the oldest and keeping the newest is the correct behavior.
